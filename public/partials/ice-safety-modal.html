Ice Safety Best Practices

Minnesota Ice Fishing Guidelines

Minimum Ice Thickness Guidelines

4"+ Clear ice for foot travel (single person)
5-7" ATVs and snowmobiles
8-12"+ Small cars or light trucks
12-15"+ Full-size pickup trucks

Ice Quality Matters

Prioritize: New, clear, blue ice for maximum strength

Avoid: White ice, candled ice, slush layers, and refrozen breaklines

Snow on ice can hide cracks, insulate weak spots, and slow freezing—don't assume thick ice under deep snow.

High-Risk Areas

Stay clear of these areas which remain unstable all season:

• Inlets & outlets
• Culverts & dams
• Channels & narrows
• River-fed bays
• Moving water
• Pressure ridges

Warning Signs

Watch for these indicators of unsafe ice:

  • • Pressure ridges and heaves
  • • Wet cracks or seeping water
  • • Ice that sounds hollow or boomy
  • • Visible cracks or dark spots
  • • Open water nearby

Essential Safety Equipment

Spud bar to test ice
Ice picks
Throw rope
Flotation gear

Check ice every few steps with a spud bar. Keep distance between people and machines.

⚠️ Early & Late Season

Early and late season ice is the most unpredictable—treat it with extra caution. Never drive fast on ice.
